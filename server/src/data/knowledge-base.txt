4. Know Your Legal Rights as a Borrower
Borrowers in India are protected by guidelines issued by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI)
regarding fair practices in debt recovery.

Some important rights include:

Right to Respectful Communication

Recovery agents must communicate with borrowers in a professional and respectful
manner. Harassment, threats, or abusive language are not permitted.

Restricted Calling Hours

Recovery agents are generally expected to contact borrowers only during reasonable
hours, typically between 7:00 AM and 7:00 PM.

No Public Humiliation

Recovery agents are not allowed to publicly shame or embarrass borrowers by
contacting unrelated individuals or spreading information about the borrower’s debt.

Identity Disclosure

Recovery agents must clearly identify themselves and the lender they represent when
contacting borrowers.

Fair Collection Practices

Collection efforts must follow ethical standards and cannot involve intimidation,
coercion, or unlawful actions.

If borrowers experience behavior that violates these guidelines, they may raise
complaints with the lender or escalate the matter through appropriate channels.

Section 5 — General Debt Repayment Strategies

These are general personal finance strategies that can help borrowers manage and repay
debt more effectively, even without enrolling in a formal program.

1. Snowball Method
The snowball method involves paying off debts starting with the smallest balance first,
while making minimum payments on all other debts.

How it works:
•   List all debts from smallest to largest balance
•   Pay as much as possible toward the smallest debt
•   Once the smallest debt is cleared, move the freed-up amount to the next smallest
•   Repeat until all debts are paid off

Why it works:
Clearing small debts quickly builds momentum and motivation. Seeing accounts close
one by one makes the process feel achievable.

Best suited for:
Borrowers who feel overwhelmed by multiple debts and need psychological wins to stay
on track.

2. Avalanche Method
The avalanche method involves paying off debts starting with the highest interest rate
first, while making minimum payments on all other debts.

How it works:
•   List all debts from highest to lowest interest rate
•   Pay as much as possible toward the highest-interest debt
•   Once that debt is cleared, move the freed-up amount to the next highest-interest debt
•   Repeat until all debts are paid off

Why it works:
This approach minimizes the total interest paid over time, saving the borrower the most
money compared to other methods.

Best suited for:
Borrowers who want to minimize total cost and are disciplined enough to stay consistent
even when progress feels slow initially.

3. Payment Prioritization
When funds are limited, prioritize payments in this order:
•   Debts with the highest days past due (to prevent legal escalation)
•   Debts with the highest interest rates (to reduce cost)
•   Debts closest to being paid off (for quick wins)

4. Direct Lender Negotiation
Borrowers can contact their lenders directly to request:
•   EMI restructuring (longer tenure, lower EMI)
•   Temporary moratorium or payment holiday
•   Waiver of late payment charges
•   Reduced interest rates for hardship cases

Lenders may be willing to negotiate, especially if the borrower demonstrates intent to
repay but lacks current capacity.

5. Budget-Based Repayment Planning
A simple budgeting approach can help borrowers allocate more toward debt:
•   Track monthly income and expenses
•   Identify non-essential spending that can be temporarily reduced

1. Credit Insights / Credit Score FAQs
What distinguishes a credit report from a credit score?

A credit report is a detailed record of your credit history, including all loans, credit cards,
repayment behaviour, outstanding balances, and enquiries made by lenders.

A credit score is a three-digit numerical summary derived from the credit report. It
indicates how likely you are to repay credit on time and helps lenders evaluate your
creditworthiness.

If I check my credit score, will it decrease?

No. Checking your own credit score is considered a soft inquiry, which does not affect
your credit score.

Only hard inquiries, such as when you apply for a loan or credit card, may temporarily
impact your score.

Will my credit score remain consistent across all credit bureaus?

No. Credit scores may differ across bureaus such as Experian, CIBIL, Equifax, and CRIF
because:

•   lenders may report data at different times
•   each bureau uses slightly different scoring models.

How frequently can my credit score change?

Your credit score can change whenever new credit information is reported to the
bureau.

Typically, lenders update records every 30–45 days, so your score may change monthly
depending on your repayment activity and credit usage.

Does my credit score get impacted by late payments?

Yes. Late payments are one of the most significant factors affecting your credit score.

Missing EMIs or credit card payments can lead to a noticeable drop in your credit score
and may remain on your credit report for several years.

Will my credit score decrease if I apply for a new credit card?

It may decrease slightly. When you apply for a credit card, the lender performs a hard
inquiry, which may temporarily reduce your score.

However, responsible usage and timely payments can improve your score over time.

Does my credit record reflect that I am a guarantor on a loan?

Yes. If you act as a guarantor on a loan, that obligation may appear in your credit report.

If the primary borrower misses payments, it can also impact your credit profile.

Why don't I have a credit score?

You may not have a credit score if:
•   you have never taken credit before
•   your credit accounts are very new
•   your credit accounts have been inactive for a long period.

Such individuals are often categorized as NTC (New to Credit).

2. Debt Resolution Program (DRP) FAQs
How does your Loan Settlement Program work?

FREED’s Debt Resolution Program is designed for people who are facing financial
hardship and are unable to repay their unsecured debts such as credit cards or personal
loans.

FREED evaluates the customer’s financial situation and negotiates with lenders to settle
debts for a reduced amount through a structured repayment process.

How do I know if loan settlement is the best option for me?

Loan settlement is typically suitable for individuals who:

•   have already missed loan payments
•   are struggling to meet EMIs
•   are facing recovery calls
•   cannot repay their loans in full.

FREED evaluates your financial profile to determine whether settlement is an
appropriate solution.

Why do we charge a Platform Fee?

The platform fee supports services provided during the program, including:

•   debt counseling
•   negotiation with lenders
•   harassment support
•   access to the FREED platform
•   credit monitoring tools
•   program management.

The fee helps ensure that the settlement process progresses effectively.

Do you guarantee that you will settle all of my loans for a certain
percentage?

No. No organization can guarantee settlement for a specific percentage because each
lender has its own policies and negotiation outcomes vary.

Settlement results depend on multiple factors including the lender, the account status,
and the customer’s repayment behaviour during the program.

Is loan settlement legitimate / is it legal?

Yes. Debt settlement is a legitimate financial solution in India for borrowers who cannot
repay their loans in full.

It involves negotiating with lenders to accept a reduced payment as full settlement of the
outstanding loan.

Who controls the Special Purpose Account where my funds are held
while I await settlement?

The funds are held in a Special Purpose Account (SPA) managed by an independent
trustee.

The customer retains control over the funds and must authorise payments when
settlements are finalized.
What is a Special Purpose Account?

A Special Purpose Account is a dedicated account used to accumulate funds that will be
used to settle debts with lenders.

Customers make monthly contributions to this account until sufficient funds are
available to negotiate settlements.

Will the loan settlement program affect my credit score?

Yes. Loan settlement can negatively impact your credit score because settled accounts
may be reported differently than fully paid accounts.

However, once debts are resolved, customers can begin rebuilding their credit profile.

Should I include all my credit cards and personal loans in the program?

This depends on your financial situation and the strategy developed during the program.

In many cases, including multiple debts can help create a more effective settlement plan.

Do interest and late fees continue after I enroll?

Interest and penalties may continue to accrue until a settlement agreement is reached
with the lender.

The goal of the program is to negotiate a final settlement that resolves the account.

10 Common Mistakes That Can Damage
Your Credit Score
Many borrowers unknowingly hurt their credit score through everyday financial behavior.
Understanding these common mistakes can help you maintain a healthy credit profile
and improve your chances of getting loans at better interest rates.
1. Missing EMI or Credit Card Payments
Payment history is the most important factor affecting your credit score.

Missing even a single EMI or credit card payment can significantly lower your score.

Example

If your credit card bill is due on the 5th of the month and you pay it on the 25th, the lender
may report the payment as late, which can negatively impact your credit score.

Tip:

Set payment reminders or enable auto-debit for EMIs.

2. Paying Only the Minimum Amount on
Credit Cards
Paying only the minimum due amount may prevent late fees, but the remaining balance
continues to accumulate interest.

Over time, this increases your credit utilization ratio, which can lower your credit score.

Example

If your credit card limit is ₹1,00,000 and your outstanding balance is ₹80,000, your
utilization ratio becomes 80%, which is considered risky by lenders.

Tip:

Try to keep credit utilization below 30–40% of your limit.
3. Using Too Much of Your Credit Limit
High credit utilization signals that you may be financially stretched.

Example

Credit Limit    Outstanding Utilization
₹1,00,000       ₹30,000     Healthy
₹1,00,000       ₹90,000     High risk

High utilization over long periods can reduce your credit score.

4. Applying for Too Many Loans or Credit
Cards
Each time you apply for credit, the lender performs a hard inquiry on your credit report.

Multiple loan applications within a short time can signal financial stress.

Example

Applying for 5 credit cards in one month may lead to multiple credit inquiries and
temporarily lower your score.

5. Closing Old Credit Cards
Older credit accounts contribute to the length of your credit history, which positively
impacts your credit score.

Closing your oldest credit card may reduce your average credit age.

Example

If your oldest credit card is 10 years old and you close it, your average credit history may
drop significantly.
6. Becoming a Guarantor for Risky Loans
When you act as a guarantor, the loan becomes part of your financial responsibility.

If the primary borrower fails to repay the loan, it may negatively affect your credit score.

Example

If a friend defaults on a loan where you are the guarantor, the lender may report the
default in your credit report.

7. Ignoring Errors in Your Credit Report
Credit reports may sometimes contain incorrect or outdated information.

Common errors include:

•   loans marked unpaid despite full repayment
•   duplicate loan entries
•   incorrect personal details.

Such errors can harm your credit score.

Tip:

Check your credit report periodically and dispute incorrect entries.

8. Defaulting or Settling Loans
Loan defaults or settlements can significantly reduce your credit score.

When a loan is marked as settled instead of fully paid, it indicates that the lender
accepted less than the full amount owed.
Example

If you settle a loan of ₹1,00,000 by paying ₹60,000, the account may be reported as
settled, which can negatively impact your credit profile.

9. Not Using Credit at All
Surprisingly, not using credit at all may also prevent you from building a strong credit
score.

If lenders cannot see any credit history, they cannot evaluate your repayment behavior.

This situation is often categorized as NTC (New to Credit).

10. Co-Signing or Joint Loans Without
Care
In joint loans or co-signed loans, both borrowers share responsibility for repayment.

If the other borrower misses payments, it can affect both credit profiles.

Example

If you take a joint home loan and your co-borrower misses EMIs, your credit score may
also be affected.

How to Maintain a Healthy Credit Score
To maintain a good credit profile:

•   Pay EMIs and credit card bills on time
•   Keep credit card utilization below 30–40%
•   Avoid applying for multiple loans simultaneously
•   Maintain older credit accounts when possible
•   Monitor your credit report regularly.

Healthy credit habits help build a strong financial profile and increase the likelihood of
loan approvals, better interest rates, and higher credit limits.
